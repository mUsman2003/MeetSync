Purpose of Phase 1
Phase 1 exists to answer one question:
If this is a real problem, could it plausibly survive as a product or business?
In Phase 0, you proved the problem exists.
In Phase 1, you attempt to disprove the business viability of addressing it.
This phase is deliberately skeptical.
Finding weaknesses is a success, not a failure.
________________


Core Mindset (Read Carefully)
* You are not pitching your idea
* You are stress-testing your idea
* You are expected to surface risks, gaps, and contradictions
In industry, strong teams kill weak ideas early.
That is the behavior this phase trains.
________________


What You Must Submit
Each team must submit one detailed presentation deck and present it in class.
The deck must include all four deliverables below.
________________


Deliverable 1 — Lean Model Canvas (Required)
Create a Lean Model Canvas (LMC) for your course project.
Your canvas must clearly define:
* Problem
* Customer Segments
* Unique Value Proposition
* Solution (high-level only)
* Channels
* Revenue Streams
* Cost Structure
* Key Metrics
* Unfair Advantage
Team Process Requirement
* One sub-team builds the initial canvas
* Another sub-team reviews it critically
You are expected to revise the canvas after critique.
________________


Deliverable 2 — Critical Analysis of the Lean Model Canvas (Required)
This is the most heavily weighted component of Phase 1.
You must explicitly identify internal mismatches and weak logic in your own canvas.
You should look for:
* Customer segment vs channel mismatches
* Revenue model vs willingness-to-pay gaps
* Key metrics that do not reflect value delivery
* Cost structures that scale faster than value
* “Unfair advantages” that are easily copied
* Solution scope that exceeds realistic constraints
You should approach this as:
* A skeptical investor
* A skeptical enterprise buyer
* A skeptical internal leadership review
Do not defend the idea blindly.
Expose where it could fail.
________________


Deliverable 3 — Commercial Viability Assessment (Required)
You must assess both Market Viability and Product Viability.
This must be explicit, structured, and evidence-backed.
________________


A. Market Viability
You must address all of the following:
* Target customer definition (user vs buyer)
* Market size or realistic proxy
* Severity and frequency of the problem
* Existing competitors and substitutes
* Switching costs and adoption barriers
* Willingness to pay (direct or indirect evidence)
You must clearly state:
* What assumptions you are making
* What evidence supports them
* Where uncertainty remains high
________________


B. Product Viability
You must address:
* Why your product would be meaningfully better than alternatives
* Whether it could realistically be built and maintained
* What differentiation could persist over time
* Risks specific to AI products (trust, accuracy, misuse, failure modes)
* Regulatory, legal, or ethical constraints (if relevant)
________________


Deliverable 4 — SMART Analysis of Business Goals (Required)
You must identify both Subjective and Objective Goals.  
As part of the Critical Analysis of the Lean Model Canvas, teams must also perform a SMART analysis of their claimed business goals. These goals should be drawn from claims already implied in the Lean Model Canvas, not newly invented targets
Teams are required to identify:
* Three objective business goals (measurable, outcome-based e.g., revenue, cost reduction, time savings, measurable efficiency)
* Three subjective business goals (perception- or value-based e.g., experience, trust, risk mitigation, reliability)
Teams must present these goals in a table format, where each goal is evaluated against SMART criteria with logical justification like represented in the sample table below:
      Goal
	    Specific
	    Measurable
	  Achievable
	   Relevant
	 Time-bound
	Increase average freelancer monthly income (Objective)
	Focuses on income earned by freelancers on the platform
	Can be measured using average monthly earnings before and after policy change
	Achievable if platform demand and skill supply remain stable
	Directly aligns with economic well-being of freelancers
	Can be evaluated over a 6–12 month period
	The purpose of this exercise is not to justify the goal, but it is used to expose vague, non-evaluable, or unjustified claims in the Lean Model Canvas. Goals that fail one or more SMART dimensions should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed.

Research Expectations (Mandatory)
You are expected to conduct light but credible research.
Acceptable sources include:
* Industry articles or reports
* Public product documentation
* Competitor pricing pages
* Reviews, forums, complaints
* Case studies or analyst blogs
Unsupported claims will be treated as assumptions.
You must be able to defend your reasoning verbally.

Common Mistakes (Read Carefully)
* Starting with “We will build an AI system that…”
* Treating AI as the value instead of the enabler
* Claiming “huge market” without segmentation
* Ignoring competitors or substitutes
* Hiding assumptions instead of exposing them
* Overselling instead of stress-testing
________________


Rules & Constraints
* Focus on business and market reality, not implementation
* No architecture diagrams
* No tech stack discussions
* No detailed feature lists
* Pivoting is allowed and encouraged if risks are severe
________________


Outcomes of Phase 1
After review, one of the following will happen:
1. Approved → proceed to Phase 2
2. Approved with Required Refinements
3. Pivot Required → revisit Phase 0 framing
Pivoting is not penalized if justified.
________________


How Phase 1 Feeds the Course Project
* Phase 2 replaces assumptions identified here with evidence
* Phase 3 builds only what survives this scrutiny
* Phase 4 evaluates whether the product deserves scaling
Weak Phase 1 work almost always causes downstream failure.
________________


Final Note to Students
A technically impressive product built on a weak business foundation is still a failure.
Phase 1 teaches you how to avoid that mistake.
